How much time do I need?

As much time as you’re willing to invest. The more time you invest, the faster you’ll learn. But even over a longer period, you can and will improve your maths and problem-solving skills.

Free Resources

There are really a lot of resources.

List of free Roadmaps and Courses

1. OSSU Curriculum Mathematics

A complete, degree-equivalent education in mathematics, crafted entirely from online materials. This structured curriculum is designed to meet the standards of an undergraduate math major, providing a clear learning path that typically spans two years. It's perfect for dedicated learners seeking a thorough and systematic foundation. It's mostly based on curses.

2. Mathematics Roadmap

Dive into mathematics through a carefully selected collection of books and exercises. This roadmap is more than a list; it's guided by a learning philosophy that emphasizes deep understanding, metacognition, and problem-solving. It’s an excellent choice for those who learn best by reading and engaging deeply with fundamental texts.

3. Self Study Map Mathematics

A thoughtfully curated collection designed to make mathematics accessible and engaging. This resource prioritizes pedagogical effectiveness and learner motivation over traditional, often overwhelming, textbooks. It's a shorter, high-quality starting point that helps you build momentum and enjoy the learning process.

4. MIT Open Courseware

MIT offers many of its mathematics courses online for free, including lecture notes, assignments, and exams. These resources are academically rigorous and ideal if you want to build a strong foundation or reach university-level proficiency in math.

5. KI Roadmap

AI topics. However, the quality can be inconsistent, and the content is often very shallow. These resources are best suited for very specific needs—not recommended for general learning or beginners.
As an example: ChatGPT Deep Research or Roadmap.sh.

Active vs Passive learning

Learning happens in two main ways: active and passive.
For a long time, I leaned too heavily on passive learning and not enough on active learning—and that was a mistake.
Passive learning is when you absorb information from external sources, such as listening to lectures or watching videos. While this approach can help you understand concepts on a deeper level, it’s almost useless if you don’t apply what you’ve learned.
That’s where active learning comes in. Active learning means engaging directly with the material—testing yourself, teaching others, participating in discussions, and solving problems. These activities turn knowledge into skills, really outwears your brain and make learning stick.

Math is not about memorizing it's about practising

How to Think of Mistakes

I used to think that making mistakes was proof that I was stupid, but in reality, mistakes are what separate good students from great ones. Imagine your math knowledge as a circle: when you encounter a problem you can’t solve, you’ve reached the edge of that circle. Once you learn how to solve it, the circle expands. Mistakes are valuable because they show you where your limits are—and every time you overcome one, you extend your knowledge.

Mental Calculation

What is Mental Calculation?

Mental calculation (or mental computation) means performing arithmetic in your mind without using tools like paper, pencil, or calculators. People use it when tools aren’t available, when it’s faster, or in competitions. It often relies on special techniques and the decimal system.

Can you flex with it?

Of course you can.
During the 18th century, it was observed that children with strong mental calculation skills often became successful scientists or mathematicians, though some faced personal challenges. Individuals who perform complex calculations quickly and accurately are considered prodigies or savants, and have been called “lightning calculators” or geniuses.
